The Compton Gamma Ray Observatory (CGRO)

The Compton Gamma Ray Observatory, often simply referred to as Compton or CGRO, was one of NASA's "Great Observatories," alongside the likes of the Hubble Space Telescope, the Chandra X-ray Observatory, and the Spitzer Space Telescope. Launched in 1991, Compton was dedicated to making observations in the gamma-ray spectrum, the highest energy form of light.

With its suite of four instruments, Compton was able to capture unprecedented details of phenomena producing gamma rays, such as supernovae, neutron stars, and black holes. Its observations have significantly expanded our understanding of these high-energy processes and the extreme conditions under which they occur.

The Compton Gamma Ray Observatory's mission lasted for nearly a decade, concluding in 2000 when it was safely deorbited after one of its gyroscopes failed. The mission's total cost was approximately $617 million, including development, launch, and operations. Despite its relatively short operational life, Compton's contributions to the field of gamma-ray astronomy were groundbreaking. Its legacy continues to influence the direction of high-energy astrophysics and stands as a testament to human curiosity and our drive to explore the unknown realms of the universe.
